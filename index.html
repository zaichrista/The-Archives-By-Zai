Bio

I hold a BA in Music from Oxford and am completing an MA in Cultural and Creative Industries at King’s College London, where my research sits at the intersection of nightlife, sound, and identity. I’ve designed runway shows, built brand worlds from scratch, coined theoretical concepts that ended up in academic papers, and directed photoshoots. The thread connecting all of it is the same obsession: why do certain spaces, sounds, and objects make people feel like themselves?

Profile

The full current.

I grew up between cultures, between disciplines, and between the feeling that I had too many interests and the slow realisation that this was the point. Music was first. Then fashion. Then the question of why certain spaces feel like they belong to you before you’ve even sat down. I’ve been trying to answer that question ever since, and the answer keeps taking me somewhere new.

I studied Music at Oxford, where my research moved from Pink Floyd’s psychoanalytic structure to the sonic architecture of intimacy and eventually to a concept I developed called sonic anti-intimacy: the engineering of disconnection through sound, the wolf in sheep’s clothing hiding inside every shopping mall playlist and noise-cancelling headphone.

For my ethnography I went further and spent three weeks in Tokyo’s underground club scene, interviewing DJs, rappers and producers in Shibuya and Shimokitazawa, and writing a 10,000-word study of how fashion, music and movement function as subcultural graffiti. I interviewed Dabo, one of the founding figures of Japanese hip hop, at a dinner table with Sade playing in the background. That is where my understanding of what a brand can do to a body was formed.

At King’s College London, my MA in Cultural and Creative Industries pushed all of that thinking into practice. My research sits at the intersection of nightlife, brand culture and the social architecture of how people gather. The entrepreneurial project that came out of it, Bekaa, became my most complete demonstration of what research-led strategy looks like when it has a room to live in.

Outside academia, I’ve designed and directed two runway shows, built brand worlds from scratch, developed a modular fashion brand around a zipper system I designed myself, directed photoshoots, and worked across hospitality marketing and creative direction. I’ve spent time at Levi’s, where I learned what it means for a brand to carry genuine cultural memory rather than just a logo people recognise.

The proof that the thinking converts: as Co-Creative Director and Co-Marketing Director of the Oxford Fashion Gala, Oxford’s largest student fashion event, I tripled ticket sales year-on-year by pairing a unified creative concept with a data-informed, multi-platform campaign. Before that, I spent six years supporting content production and photoshoots for Mandaloun, a Lebanese hospitality brand in Westfield London, and I’ve run a bar of my own — managing operations and designing the seasonal cocktail programme at The Reach Brasserie. When I write about hospitality, atmosphere and cultural strategy, it comes from rooms I have actually worked in.

That rooms-I-have-worked-in knowledge is also what I now sell. I run ZC Studios, a creative strategy and branding practice, where I take brand theory into paying client work: positioning, content systems and campaigns for hospitality and lifestyle brands, including Baba G, Mandaloun and The Reach Brasserie.
